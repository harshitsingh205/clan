Legacy of Stories

Some stories are never written in history books. They are neither about kings and emperors, nor about victories in battles. These stories are hidden in our village lanes, in temple walls, and in the words of our elders. As some grandmother or grandfather says - "It may not be written, but we remember."

The Living Saga of Narauni

The story of the Naravani (Narauni) of Narendrapur is also such a memory. This is no ordinary story, it is a living heritage that has been preserved generation after generation. This is not just about one family, it is the story of people who were once in forts, then reached farmlands, passed through forests and settled in distant villages. This is their journey, which continues to this day.

Struggle, Hope and Honor

This booklet does not merely provide details of one family. It contains tales of olden struggles, courage, the sorrow of being away from home, and the hope of building a new home. It honors those stories that our forefathers preserved in their hearts and that we are now writing down and carrying forward.

For Future Generations

The matters written in this book are not merely about past times. These are guiding words for the coming generation, so that they may know who their family is and where they came from. This heritage is a source of inspiration for them, enabling them to take pride in their past and determine the direction of their future.

The Beginning

This is just the beginning - of preserving memories, understanding them, and passing them forward. This is the story of all those who kept this heritage alive, and for all those who will carry it forward. This is the story of Narauni - a story that has not ended yet, but new chapters are being written.

Narwar Garh: The Birthplace of Narauni Lineage

Every lineage has a beginning, a point where history, tradition, and identity take shape together. Similarly, the Narauni Rajput clan originates from the historic Narwar Fort in present-day Madhya Pradesh. A fort that symbolized valor, administration, and cultural dignity. In the shadow of this fort lived a clan of Rajput warriors, who later came to be known as "Naravani" and in local pronunciation as "Narauni".

The Story of Naming

Their name came from their residence in Narwar Garh. This is not an ordinary surname, but a memory - of that fort, that land, and that lifestyle that remained with them for centuries. This name not only signifies a place but encompasses an entire cultural heritage preserved generation after generation.

Lineage Tradition and Gotra

According to traditional sources, the Narauni clan is considered a branch of the "Shrineta Vansham", a lineage known for foresight, policy, and traditional decorum. This clan primarily has the Kashyap gotra, but some branches are also connected to Vashisht or Bharadvaj gotras. This gotra system not only holds religious significance but also represents social structure and family relationships.

Characteristics of the Lineage

The identity of the Narauni clan was not limited to Rajput valor alone. The distinctive feature of this clan was social wisdom, administrative skill, and cultural sensitivity. This is why when they had to leave Narwar Garh and move eastward, they carried not only their military strength but also their cultural heritage and administrative experience.

Legacy of Values

This heritage from Narwar Garh was not merely material. It included values that proved helpful in establishing the Narauni clan in new territories - sense of justice, responsibility towards society, ability for cultural harmonization, and faith in traditions. It was these values that transformed this clan from merely a military force into a socio-cultural force.

Living Heritage Even Today

Even today when people of the Narauni clan speak about their origin, the name Narwar Garh is taken with pride. This is not merely a historical fact but an identity, a selfhood that has continued for centuries. This heritage has given the Narauni clan a distinctive identity that defines their history, their struggles, and their achievements.

Departure: A New Direction Under the Guise of Pilgrimage

Approximately 300 years ago, two ancestors of the Narauni clan, Shri Durg Dev and Shri Labhang Dev, set out eastward under the pretext of pilgrimage. At that time, Rajput leaders traveled with armed soldiers - for self-protection and to establish their lineage and dominance by conquering new territories if opportunities arose. These journeys were not merely religious but also attempts to move toward a new social and geographical identity.

Through Uttarakhand to Ballia

Traveling through the hills of Uttarakhand and the pilgrimage sites of Garhwal, these people reached Ballia district. During this journey, they visited sacred places like Badrinath Dham and Langoor Gadhi, Bhairav Gadhi temple in Uttarakhand. This was not merely a physical journey but a spiritual and cultural journey that introduced them to the culture and lifestyle of new regions.

First Settlement in Ballia

In Ballia district, they established their forts in villages like Devdhi, Bansdih, Sukhpura, and Raja Gaon Kharauni. It was here that their identity as 'Naravani' (Narauni) was established. Even today, this line is heard in local sayings: "Raja Gaon, Kharauni jahan base teen sau ghar Narauni." This saying makes it clear how strong a social and administrative grip the Narauni family had established in this region.

Leadership of Babu Vanshidhar Rai

Among these branches emerged a prominent warrior, Babu Vanshidhar Rai, who set a new example of social leadership. His nine sons established forts in Bharathui, Chhitampur, Lohgajar, Narendrapur, Selrapur, Sarharwa, Vishnupur, Jamapur, and other villages, creating an extensive socio-political framework. This expansion was not only geographical but also cultural and social.

Cultural Harmonization and Identity

The Narauni clan excelled not only in warfare skills but also in social wisdom and respect for local culture. In Uttarakhand, they became known as 'Rawat', while in Purvanchal and Bihar, along with zamindari, they spread justice, service, and agricultural culture. Wherever they resided, they adopted the local language, culture, and lifestyle, but never abandoned their roots and identity.

Foundation of Narendrapur

According to local traditions, Narendrapur was founded around 1715 CE. The branch coming from Ballia, Asao, and Pachlakh parganas, which included decisive ancestors like Shakti Rai, settled here. The village's original name was "Narinpur", which over time changed to "Narendrapur", and even today this first name lives on the tongues of elders.

Legacy of the Journey

This great journey became an integral part of the Narauni clan's identity. The journey from Narwar Garh to Narendrapur not only covered geographical distance but also faced cultural and social transformations. Today, the winds of Narendrapur echo this long journey. And in the words of elders, there is that invisible thread that connects the past with the present. This journey was not merely a family's journey but a journey of tradition, culture, and identity that continues to this day.

Resistance Against Colonial Taxation System

When the East India Company attempted to collect taxes from Andar and Pachlakh parganas, the leadership of the Narauni clan rejected this unjust system. Babu Veera Rai, the youngest son of Vanshidhar Rai, raised his voice against this tax regime. This was not ordinary opposition but a calculated strategy to protect the rights of farmers and villagers.

Attack on Asao Fort and Retaliation

The then ruler of Siwan, Ali Khan, attacked Asao Fort but had to retreat in the face of Veera Rai's retaliation. This was not merely a military victory but a symbol of protecting local autonomy and self-respect. Veera Rai demonstrated that standing up for justice and opposing injustice is the fundamental right of any community.

Dirga Rai's Armed Resistance

After Veera Rai's death, his son Dirga Rai took over administrative responsibilities. He too rejected the British tax system. When the English administration ignored his warnings and sent a small military contingent to Mairewa, Dirga Rai attacked at night with his soldiers and eliminated the entire unit. According to some accounts, he hung the heads of killed soldiers on his fort walls - a strategy of psychological as well as military resistance.

Betrayal and Transportation to Andaman

Angered by this incident, the British sent a large army, but around the same time, Dirga Rai was afflicted with paralysis. Due to his weakness, he had to leave the fort. Unfortunately, on information from some members of his own clan, he was captured and sentenced to Kalapani (transportation to Andaman). He was childless, but the memory of his courage still lives with pride among his descendants.

Dhajju Singh's Political Intervention

During this period of struggle, Dhajju Singh of the Bharwui Garh branch played a significant role in the history of Hathua State. When Hathua's King Basant Sahi was murdered by his own brother Fateh Sahi, the widowed queen reached Bharhui Garh with her newborn son Mahesh Sahi and sought refuge from Dhajju Singh. The Narauni family not only provided them security but later arranged Mahesh Sahi's marriage to the ruler of Chainpur.

Diplomatic Efforts and Establishment of Justice

Dhajju Singh corresponded with the British administration to attempt to restore Mahesh Sahi's lost kingdom. This correspondence was an example of legal as well as political strategy. Unfortunately, Mahesh Sahi died at a young age, but posthumously, his son Chatradhari Sahi eventually obtained the throne of Hathua. This support was motivated by the spirit of protecting justice and righteousness.

Martyr Umakant Singh's Sacrifice

During the freedom struggle, the Narauni clan made another significant contribution. Shri Umakant Singh, also known as Amar Shahid Veer Raman, was a courageous freedom fighter. During the 1942 'Quit India Movement', while he was studying in Patna with his brother, he participated in the campaign to hoist the tricolor on the Secretariat on August 11, 1942. The British government's Gorkha soldiers opened fire, and in this conflict, Umakant Singh became a martyr.

Struggle Alive in Memories

In memory of Umakant Singh's sacrifice, the Martyr Memorial in Patna and the special memorial built in Narendrapur still tell the story of his bravery. This sacrifice became a source of inspiration not only for one family but for the entire region. His struggle symbolizes active participation in social consciousness and rural development.

The Trilogy of Struggle: Opposition, Resistance, and Sacrifice

The struggle history of the Narauni clan can be seen as a trilogy: Veera Rai's stand against colonial system, Dirga Rai's armed resistance, and Umakant Singh's sacrifice in the freedom struggle. These three chapters demonstrate that the struggle for justice and freedom can take various forms - sometimes as administrative opposition, sometimes as military resistance, and sometimes as sacrifice.

Continuity of Heritage

Even today, the ruins of Asao Fort, Narendrapur's Martyr Memorial, and family memories bear witness to this struggle. This history is not merely about the past but serves as guidance for the present generation. It teaches that standing up for justice, opposing injustice, and protecting freedom is the duty of every generation. This struggle transformed the Narauni clan from merely a family into an ideology - an ideology of self-respect, justice, and freedom.

Distinguished Contributors

Over many generations of the Narauni lineage, some names emerged who gave direction to family and society through their thoughts, actions, and dedication. These are the people whose memory still lives in village lanes, monuments, and stories.

  • Shakti Rai: Built the fort in Narendrapur and expanded the Narauni community in Narendrapur.
  • Sitaram Singh: Constructed the Shiva temple, other temples, and ponds in Narendrapur, initiating permanent heritage in Narendrapur.
  • Shiv Kumar Singh: Devotee of music; his music gatherings became part of the village's cultural life.
  • Krishna Kumar Singh: Highly respected Ayurvedic expert of Narendrapur. Had special interest in travel and pilgrimage, and visited Badrinath Dham.
  • Suba Singh: Among those individuals of Narendrapur village who had complete knowledge of every piece of village land. His efficiency in amin work strengthened village administration and land management.
  • Rajnandan Singh: Popularly known as 'Babua Kaka', remembered for cooperative nature and love for agriculture.
  • Parsanay Singh: Freedom fighter who actively participated in the 1942 movement and awakened rural consciousness.
  • Rajanarayan Singh: First postmaster of Narendrapur village. His contribution in connecting the village with the outside world through postal system was important.
  • Bacchu Singh: Known for his sharp temperament and deep political understanding. His grasp of rural politics and decision-making ability influenced the village's collective life.
  • Shambhu Prasad Singh: Scholar associated with higher judiciary; his historical works became heritage of the lineage.
  • Dashrath Singh: Serious by nature but skilled player of internal politics and strategies. His role in village's strategic activities is considered special.
  • Vishwanath Singh: Person of simple and straightforward nature. His personality clearly reflected affection and sociability.
  • Rudrapratap Singh: Served as uncontested village head for years, setting an example of service and harmony.
  • Umakant Prasad Singh: Martyred in the Quit India Movement; his sacrifice still resides in the village's soul.
  • Surya Singh: Cheerful and humorous nature along with being hot-tempered. His presence in village social life was always influential.
  • Samundra Singh: Gentle, humble, and festive-loving. Promoted social events and harmony. Extremely skilled in singing Holi songs.
  • Mutri Singh: Worked at Patna High Court and remained famous as respected and reliable person due to simple straightforward nature.
  • Ramadhar Singh: Proficient in English, theater lover, and respected villager who adopted Gandhian lifestyle.
  • Dr. Faneesh Singh: Dedicated seeker of literature, history, and education; played leading role in village's cultural upliftment.
  • Shyamdhata Singh: Though associated with contracting profession, had deep interest in cultural activities. Popular identity formed due to active participation especially in drama and cultural events.
  • Dhrup Singh: Enthusiastic, energetic person who promoted social harmony. Active in both sports and drama. Extremely sociable nature and maintained friendship with three generations simultaneously.
  • Chandrashekhar Singh: Served dedicatedly as rural doctor for village's health life. His simple and sociable nature made him beloved to all.
  • Suryashekhar Singh: Simple and straightforward natured person who remained popular in village due to his humorous and sociable personality.
  • Raghvendra Singh: Connected with spirit of service and dialogue; established identity in village as follower of Paras Babu.
  • Kamleendra Kumar Singh: Educationist and history lover; contributed to preserving village memory and education.
  • Madhavendra Kumar Singh: Skilled in business and promoter of cultural activities in village; extremely gentle and affectionate natured person.
  • Chandrakirti Singh: Martyr's son, creative and inspired by Gandhian thinking - always remained leading in cultural events.
  • Manish Prasad Singh: Advocate and social worker; continuously worked for education and social consciousness.
  • Captain Harikirti Singh: Retired army captain, deeply connected to village and beloved to all due to sociable nature.
  • Shivkirti Singh: Former Supreme Court judge; his judicial journey became inspiration for both lineage and region.
